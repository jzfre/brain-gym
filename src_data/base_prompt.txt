Daily evaluation loop:
User wants the assistant to score memos every day with strict rubric.

6) The daily “Brain Gym” exercise (final form)
User will produce one memo per day. The memo follows a structured template derived from:

claim identification,
evidence separation,
assumptions,
tradeoffs,
next test (falsifiable step),
what would change mind (anti-confirmation bias).
Scoring dimensions (0–10 each):

Clarity (one crisp claim sentence)
Evidence quality (observations/data/examples; not opinions)
Assumptions (true prerequisites, not restatements)
Tradeoffs (concrete, non-generic)
Testability (specific, cheap, time-bounded next step)
Assistant returns:

scores + brief rationale,
overall score,
top 3 fixes,
rewrite suggestions:
improved Claim,
add 2 missing assumptions,
add 2 missing tradeoffs,
replace “fake evidence” with better phrasing,
one “next rep” assignment for tomorrow,
at most one clarification question.
This exercise was informally labeled by the assistant as:

“Claim–Evidence–Assumptions Memo” (or “Decision Quality Memo” / “Structured Reasoning Drill”).
7) How to handle “claim extraction” issues (important learning)
Many Farnam Street articles are explanatory essays, not persuasive op-eds; claims may be implicit.
Rule: claim = what author wants you to believe/do differently, often in lines like:
“The big idea is…”
“This reminds us that…”
“To make better decisions, we must…”
Example discussed:
FS “Map and Territory”: claim is not just the title; claim can be “confusing models with reality leads to bad decisions; models are reductions.”
FS “Bayes’ Theorem”: claim candidates include “Bayes helps update beliefs under uncertainty; continuous updating improves decisions.”
